# Academic Expression

Use this file when polishing, translating, or drafting wildfire/fire-refuge
academic prose, captions, figure legends, titles, abstracts, related work, or
results paragraphs.

## Core Stance

- Fix argument before wording.
- Do not invent data, citations, mechanisms, novelty, or policy authority.
- Keep wildfire safety and policy claims bounded by jurisdiction and evidence.
- Preserve useful English technical terms, especially in Chinese explanations.
- Use hedging when evidence is observational, model-based, geography-specific,
  or policy-contingent.

## Section Moves

### Abstract

Use:

`context -> gap -> approach -> key evidence -> implication -> boundary`

For method or AI work:

`decision problem -> data/method gap -> model/workflow -> validation -> use case -> limitation`

### Introduction

Use:

`field scale -> hazard or ecological importance -> current knowledge -> unresolved gap -> study aim`

For fire refuge/refugia topics, define the term before the gap.

### Related Work

Group by theme:

- wildfire hazard and risk
- human refuge, evacuation, and WUI planning
- ecological fire refugia and recovery
- remote sensing and GIS evidence
- AI/modeling and uncertainty
- policy, governance, and community resilience

Avoid paper-by-paper chronology unless the user asks for history.

### Results

Write claim-first paragraphs:

1. claim sentence
2. data or comparison
3. quantitative or spatial evidence
4. exception, uncertainty, or boundary
5. link to next result

Do not drift into broad interpretation too early. Save larger meaning for
Discussion.

### Discussion

Use:

`central finding -> relation to prior work -> explanation -> planning/ecological implication -> limitation -> future work`

For safety-relevant topics, separate scientific implication from operational
recommendation.

## Useful Phrase Families

Use these as patterns, not fixed phrases:

- Definition: "Here, we use [term] to refer to [scope], rather than [nearby concept]."
- Gap: "However, less is known about [gap] at [scale/context]."
- Evidence: "Using [data/method], we found that [result] was associated with [driver/condition]."
- Spatial pattern: "[Pattern] was concentrated in [setting], whereas [contrast] occurred in [setting]."
- Uncertainty: "This pattern should be interpreted cautiously because [limitation]."
- Transferability: "The finding is likely to transfer to [context] only where [conditions] hold."
- Policy boundary: "These results inform [planning question], but they do not replace local emergency guidance."
- Ecological boundary: "Unburned patches were treated as candidate refugia unless functional persistence was directly assessed."

## Caption Expression

Caption sentences should do more than name the plot:

- first sentence: main message
- second sentence: data, geography, and time
- third sentence: panel guide or metric definition
- final sentence: uncertainty, limitation, or implication

Avoid captions that only say "Map of study area" or "Results of model".

## Title Patterns

Choose concrete, searchable titles:

- "[Driver] shapes [fire refugia pattern] in [ecosystem/region]"
- "Mapping [wildfire outcome] with [data/method] across [scale]"
- "[Evacuation/refuge constraint] limits [community safety outcome] in [WUI context]"
- "Remote sensing reveals [post-fire recovery/refugia pattern] after [fire regime]"

Avoid overbroad titles such as "A new framework for wildfire resilience" unless
the evidence really supports a framework-level contribution.

## Chinese-to-English Workflow

For Chinese drafts:

1. Extract core propositions.
2. Identify section job.
3. Reorder ideas to match English academic logic.
4. Preserve technical terms.
5. Add hedging and boundaries.
6. Polish sentence flow.

Return polished English first when the user asks for English writing, followed
by brief Chinese notes explaining major structural choices.
